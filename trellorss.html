An unecessary and probably extremely boring description of the dev process for this app. I take no responsiblity for the health of those that read this.

So, Trello! Great stuff. This was the first "ToDo" app (okay, I'm sure the folks over at Fog Creek would cringe at me calling it a ToDo app, so sorry ahead of time) that I've used that was unintrusive enough for me not to require another ToDo app in order to just manage my ToDo apps. I've been using it for nearly about 16 months now, and I have over a dozen boards. (And the satisfaction of moving a card I've been working on into the 'Done' list has become a reward in and of itself these days. Simple pleasures.)

Anyway, Trello doesn't have a built in RSS feed feature. It's something that has been requested several times. Since I thought it would be particularly nice to have RSS feeds for several of my boards, I decided to implement it myself. Which, apparently is all part of Joel Spolsky's master plan anyway:

Another way of putting that is: never build anything in-house if you can expose a basic API and get those high-value users (the ones who are getting the most value out of the platform) to build it for you. On the Trello team, any feature that can be provided by a plug-in must be provided by a plug-in.

Anyway, having RSS feeds for your Trello boards has a few advantages. It's most useful when you have several group or public boards you are keeping track of. Trello has email notifications, or you can just keep track of the progress of the board on the board activity bar. However, if you're following multiple boards it can be easier and more convenient to have a set of feeds that go into the RSS reader you already use.

The TrelloRSS module is simple enough. It utilizes PyRSS2Gen to generate the actual feeds, and uses Py-Trello to interface with the Trello API. All you do is create a TrelloRSS object, define whether you want to read from public or private boards, and then pass in the types of and number of actions to follow. This is handy, for instance, if you just want a feed to keep track of new cards that people make. Or just new comments, or whatever.

After releasing it, a few people expressed interest in there being a web app that they could create and manage their feeds from. So I whipped up a quick app on Google App Engine to do just that.

There's a couple of interesting things about the app. When the user creates a feed, it just stores a feed entity in the datastore that has all the necessary info to retrieve the feed from Trello with a TrelloRSS object. I don't ever store any info from the actual feeds inside the datastore itself -- that's all done via memcache. When the info from a feed is requested, it retrieves the info from Trello and stores it in the datastore with a 1/2 hour expiration. Most RSS feeds update every 30 minutes or an hour, so that's about how often a feed's info really needs to be updated. If a feed is queried but we don't have it in memcache, another query is made to the Trello API.

This works pretty well for the most part, although I have seen a few feeds timeout when they were retrieving a ton of info from Trello. (Like, 12+ boards worth of info in one request.) It's been pretty rare though.

I've thought about rewriting it to use Trello's webhooks instead (and I even added Webhook support to Py-Trello in anticipation of this), but the payoff just isn't there. If there were a lot more people using this app, and a lot more feeds, I could see the benefit of feeds being automatically updated whenever a change is made on the Trello side. However, with the pretty minimal use it gets, the current structure works fine.

There are currently about 50 people using TrelloRSS. It makes me happy to know it's been useful for at least some people.

...and then I found out later that Zapier already has Trello-RSS feed features. Oh well! At least mine is open source. And free.
